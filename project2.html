Improving Health Insurance Literacy for UCI Students

A collaborative UX design project focused on making healthcare information more accessible and understandable for college students.

Overview

Project Type:
UX Research + Product Design
Team:
5 Designers
Timeline:
10 weeks

Project Overview

This project was completed as part of a class assignment in collaboration with six other students. Our goal was to design a digital solution that helps UC Irvine students better understand their health insurance options and make more informed healthcare decisions.

Through research, ideation, and design, we explored how simplified language, clear comparisons, and student-centered guidance could improve health insurance literacy and reduce confusion around coverage.

The Problem

Many college students at UC Irvine struggle to understand their health insurance options. While resources may exist, much of the information is difficult to access, filled with complex terminology, or scattered across multiple platforms.

Our Solution

We designed a student-centered digital platform that simplifies health insurance concepts, supports comparison, and helps students make informed decisions without pressure.

My Role

  • Conducted user research and competitive analysis to identify key pain points.
  • Contributed to ideation sessions and storyboard creation.
  • Designed mid- to high-fidelity prototypes focused on clarity and usability.
  • Participated in user testing and iteration based on feedback.

Research & Competitive Analysis

Our competitive analysis revealed a consistent pattern across existing health insurance platforms: most assume users already understand insurance basics or push them toward enrollment before they feel confident. These gaps raised an important question— how do college students actually experience and understand health insurance today?

To validate these observations and ensure our design addressed real student needs, we conducted research focused on students’ insurance literacy, confidence, and decision-making behaviors when navigating healthcare systems.

Research Methods

User research, competitive analysis, usability testing

Key Insights

  • Students lack foundational knowledge of insurance concepts
  • Complex terminology creates confusion and anxiety
  • Users feel overwhelmed and disengage from existing platforms
  • Early requests for personal data reduce trust and increase drop-off

~50% of students reported confusion when selecting or using their insurance, with some delaying care due to uncertainty.

Research Questions

  • What baseline understanding do college students have about health insurance?
  • Which insurance terms, concepts, or plan details cause the most confusion?
  • How do students currently seek out health insurance information?
  • At what points do students feel overwhelmed or disengage from existing platforms?
  • What type of guidance helps students feel informed without feeling pressured to commit?

Design Implications

  1. Education-First Experience

    2. Allow students to explore and learn without providing personal information upfront.

  2. Progressive Disclosure

    3. Introduce information in manageable chunks to reduce cognitive load and prevent overwhelm.

  3. Minimal Data Collection

    4. Only collect necessary information to avoid premature commitment and reduce user anxiety.

  4. Guided Comparison Flow

    5. Ensure a seamless transition to official enrollment platforms with clear communication about the next steps.

Designing the Experience

To translate our research into a meaningful product experience, we designed a user flow that guides students from initial confusion to confident decision-making. Rather than overwhelming users upfront, the flow prioritizes learning, clarity, and gradual progression.

This approach was grounded in a key insight: students don’t just need access to information—they need guidance in understanding it. As a result, we structured the experience to support users step-by-step as they build confidence.

Key Design Principles

  • Education before personalization: Helps reduce anxiety and builds foundational understanding
  • Minimal data collection: Avoids early commitment and increases trust
  • Progressive disclosure: Breaks down complex information into manageable steps
  • Clear handoff to providers: Supports a seamless transition to real enrollment platforms

Exploring the Experience

Before moving into interface design, we explored how students might experience the platform over time through storyboards. This allowed us to focus on the user journey—how someone unfamiliar with insurance could gradually build understanding and confidence—without being constrained by layout decisions.

We tested different entry points and levels of guidance to better understand how structure and pacing could influence engagement and reduce confusion.

Storyboard Directions

An education-first approach where users learn key insurance concepts before exploring plan options.

A guided comparison flow that supports decision-making after users build foundational knowledge.

Bringing the Solution to Life

Building on these explorations, we developed mid- to high-fidelity prototypes that emphasized clarity, structure, and ease of use. The interface was designed to simplify complex information through visual hierarchy, step-by-step guidance, and approachable language.

Each screen was intentionally designed to reduce cognitive load while helping users feel more confident navigating their healthcare choices.

Validating the Design

Through user testing, we evaluated how effectively the design supported understanding and decision-making. While users responded positively to the structured flow, testing revealed areas where terminology and context could be further simplified.

Based on this feedback, we refined language, improved navigation clarity, and adjusted content organization to better align with users’ mental models.

Impact & Takeaways

This project demonstrated how thoughtful UX design can make complex systems more accessible. By shifting the focus from enrollment to education, we created an experience that empowers users rather than overwhelms them.

It also reinforced the importance of designing for real-world comprehension—especially in high-stakes domains where clarity directly impacts user well-being.

Reflection

This project deepened my understanding of how UX design can make complex, high-stakes information more accessible. Designing for health insurance literacy challenged me to think beyond usability and focus on comprehension—how users learn, process, and build confidence when navigating unfamiliar systems.

I learned that reducing cognitive load isn’t just about simplifying interfaces, but about structuring information in a way that aligns with users’ mental models. Prioritizing education before action proved critical in reducing anxiety and helping users feel more in control of their decisions.
