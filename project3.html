ROAM: Gamifying Local Exploration

A mobile app that reduces decision fatigue by guiding users through curated, gamified local experiences.

Overview

Project Type:
UX Research + Interaction Design
Team:
7 Designers
Timeline:
8 weeks
Focus:
Exploration + Social Play

The Problem

Young adults want to explore their cities but face decision paralysis due to:

  • Overwhelming options
  • Lack of personalized recommendations
  • Difficulty discovering local gems
  • Limited social exploration tools
As a result, users default to repetitive routines instead of discovering new local experiences.

Our Solution

We designed ROAM, a mobile app that transforms local exploration into guided, gamified experiences. Instead of searching endlessly, users can:

  • Join personalized scavenger hunts
  • Discover hidden gems through interactive maps
  • Earn rewards for trying new activities
  • Share adventures with friends

My Role

As a UX Designer on the ROAM team, I focused on interaction design and validation, specifically:

  • Led usability testing and synthesis
  • Identified key friction points in early flows
  • Drove iteration on navigation and roam creation experience
  • Contributed to wireframes and high-fidelity prototypes in Figma

Target Audience

  • Young adults seeking new experiences
  • People new to an area
  • Social explorers & food/cafe enthusiasts
  • Families planning weekend activities

User Research & Surveys

Survey Size: 33 respondents

About 60% of respondents use Google Maps, Yelp, TikTok, or Instagram to discover activities — but none provide guided, interactive outings.

    Key Insight 1: Discovery is fragmented

  • Users jump between multiple platforms to plan one outing, creating friction and drop-off.

    • Key Insight 2: Too many choices

  • Users feel overwhelmed and avoid making decisions, leading to repetitive behavior

    • Key Insight 3: Desire for social, shareable experiences

  • Users are more likely to try new things with others.

Personas

Mary Park — Student Explorer (Age 20)

  • Cafe hopping & social outings
  • Finds spots through TikTok
  • Feels bored with repeat places
  • Wants personalized local discovery

Competitive Analysis

We evaluated Yelp, Google Maps, TikTok, Beli, Let’s Roam, and others.

  • Strong at search & reviews
  • Weak in gamification
  • Limited social exploration
  • No guided real-world adventure flow

ROAM fills this gap with guided, gamified, social exploration.

Information Architecture

  • Home Dashboard
  • Create / Join Roam
  • Interactive Map
  • Feed (social posts)
  • Rewards & Points
  • Leaderboard
  • Profile & Settings
  • Invites & Notifications

Wireframes → Iterations

Low- and mid-fidelity wireframes explored scavenger hunt flows, map progress tracking, and social invitations.

Key early issues discovered:

  • Low feature discoverability
  • Roam creation confusion
  • Unclear navigation

Usability Testing Findings

  1. 50%+ of users expressed strong interest in guided exploration tools
  2. Improved task success and navigation clarity after iteration
  3. Validated demand for social, structured local discovery experiences

Changes Implemented

  • Introduced clear primary CTAs for starting Roams
  • Added onboarding tips to highlight key features
  • Enhanced feedback for user actions (e.g., confirmations, progress)

Branding & Design System

  • Warm exploration-focused palette
  • IBM Plex Sans typography system
  • Mascot + badge system
  • Gamified reward visuals

Final Prototype

  • Scavenger-hunt style roams
  • Personalized recommendations
  • Interactive map + progress tracking
  • Points & rewards system
  • Social feed & co-op hunts
  • Leaderboards & badges

Future Enhancements

  • Tiered rewards & streak bonuses
  • Co-op team hunts
  • Multi-day roam planning
  • Lodging & travel integrations
  • Weekly/monthly challenges

Reflection

This project shifted my approach from designing features to designing systems of behavior. I learned how:

  • Reducing choice can improve engagement
  • Structure can be more valuable than flexibility
  • Social motivation is a powerful driver of user action
Most importantly, I learned how to translate ambiguous user needs into clear product decisions backed by research and iteration.
